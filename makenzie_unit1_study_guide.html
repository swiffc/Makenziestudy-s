üî¨ TEKS 8.7A: Newton's Second Law

What you'll learn: How to calculate and analyze acceleration based on net force and mass

F = m √ó a
Force = mass √ó acceleration

üéØ Key Concepts

1. Net Force: The sum of all forces acting on an object, taking direction into account. Forces in the same direction add together; forces in opposite directions subtract.

Example:

A box has 50 N pushing right and 20 N friction pushing left.

Net Force = 50 N - 20 N = 30 N to the right

2. The Relationship: Acceleration is directly proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass.
  • More force ‚Üí More acceleration
  • More mass ‚Üí Less acceleration
  • Acceleration points in the same direction as net force
3. Rearranging the Formula:
  • To find acceleration: a = F √∑ m
  • To find force: F = m √ó a
  • To find mass: m = F √∑ a
4. Units:
  • Force: Newtons (N)
  • Mass: kilograms (kg)
  • Acceleration: meters per second squared (m/s¬≤)

Problem 3: If the net force on an object doubles but mass stays the same, what happens to acceleration?
Answer: Acceleration doubles.
Why: Since a = F/m, if F doubles and m stays the same, then a must double. They are directly proportional.
Problem 4: If mass doubles but net force stays the same, what happens to acceleration?
Answer: Acceleration is cut in half (halves).
Why: Since a = F/m, if m doubles and F stays the same, then a is divided by 2. They are inversely proportional.

üî¨ TEKS 8.7B: Newton's Three Laws of Motion

What you'll learn: How Newton's three laws work together in real-world systems

üéØ Key Concepts

Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia):

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, UNLESS acted upon by an unbalanced (net) force.

  • Inertia: The tendency of objects to resist changes in motion
  • More mass = more inertia = harder to start or stop
  • Real-world example: Seatbelts! When a car stops suddenly, your body keeps moving forward (inertia) until the seatbelt applies a force to stop you.
Newton's Second Law (F = ma):

The acceleration of an object depends on the net force acting on it and its mass.

  • This is what you learned in 8.7A!
  • Real-world example: It takes more force to accelerate a loaded truck than an empty one at the same rate.
Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction):

For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force.

  • Forces always come in pairs
  • The forces are equal in size but opposite in direction
  • The forces act on DIFFERENT objects
  • Real-world example: When you push on the ground backward (action), the ground pushes you forward (reaction) - that's how you walk!

üìù Practice Problems - 8.7B

Problem 1: You're on a skateboard and push the ground backward. You move forward. Which law explains this?
Answer: Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)
Explanation: You push ground backward (action) ‚Üí Ground pushes you forward (reaction). These are equal and opposite forces on different objects.
Problem 2: Why does a heavier football player need more force to achieve the same acceleration as a lighter player?
Answer: Newton's Second Law (F = ma)
Explanation: Since a = F/m, if mass (m) is bigger, you need a bigger force (F) to get the same acceleration (a). More mass requires more force.
Problem 3: A book sits on a table. Why doesn't it fall through the table?
Answer: Newton's Third Law
Explanation: The book pushes down on the table (action) with its weight. The table pushes up on the book (reaction) with an equal force. These balanced forces keep the book at rest.
Problem 4: You're in a car that suddenly brakes. You lurch forward. Which law explains this?
Answer: Newton's First Law (Inertia)
Explanation: Your body was in motion with the car. When the car stops (force applied to car), your body wants to keep moving forward at constant velocity until the seatbelt applies a force to stop you.
Problem 5: A rocket accelerates upward during launch. When does this happen?
Answer: When the upward thrust force is greater than the downward forces (weight + air resistance).
Explanation: Newton's Second Law - there must be a net upward force to create upward acceleration. Thrust > (Weight + Drag) = Net force up = Acceleration up.

üî¨ TEKS 8.10A: Sun, Hydrosphere, & Atmosphere

What you'll learn: How energy from the Sun interacts with water and air to create weather and climate

üéØ Key Concepts

1. The Sun is the Energy Source:

The Sun provides energy that drives ALL weather and climate on Earth through uneven heating.

  • Equator receives more direct sunlight ‚Üí warmer
  • Poles receive less direct sunlight ‚Üí colder
  • This temperature difference drives wind and ocean currents
2. The Water Cycle Connection:

The Sun's energy powers the water cycle, which links the hydrosphere (water) and atmosphere (air).

  • Evaporation: Sun heats water ‚Üí water vapor rises into atmosphere
  • Condensation: Water vapor cools ‚Üí forms clouds
  • Precipitation: Water falls back to Earth as rain, snow, sleet, or hail
3. Convection - How Heat Moves:

Convection is the transfer of heat through the movement of fluids (liquids and gases).

  • Warm air/water is less dense ‚Üí rises
  • Cool air/water is more dense ‚Üí sinks
  • This creates a circular motion called a convection current
  • Example: Sea breeze during the day, land breeze at night

4. Weather vs. Climate:
  • Weather: Day-to-day conditions (temperature, precipitation, wind, clouds) in a specific place
  • Climate: Average weather patterns over 30+ years in a region
  • Example: "It's raining today" = weather. "Seattle is rainy" = climate.
5. Pressure Systems:

Why? Rising air cools ‚Üí condensation ‚Üí clouds/rain. Sinking air warms ‚Üí evaporation ‚Üí clear skies.

6. Weather Fronts:

A front is the boundary between two different air masses.

  • Cold Front: Cold air pushes under warm air ‚Üí tall clouds ‚Üí heavy storms ‚Üí cooler after
  • Warm Front: Warm air slides over cold air ‚Üí layered clouds ‚Üí steady light rain ‚Üí warmer after
  • Stationary Front: Neither air mass moves ‚Üí clouds linger for days
  • Occluded Front: Cold front catches up to warm front ‚Üí complex weather

7. Oceans Moderate Climate:
  • Water has high specific heat ‚Üí heats/cools slowly
  • Coastal areas have milder temperatures than inland areas
  • Ocean currents transport heat around the planet
Remember: Low pressure = rising air = clouds and rain. High pressure = sinking air = clear and dry. This is a common test question!

üìù Practice Problems - 8.10A

Problem 1: Why are coastal cities like San Francisco milder in temperature than inland cities at the same latitude?
Answer: Water has high specific heat capacity and heats/cools slowly. Ocean currents also transport heat. This moderates (makes milder) the temperature of coastal areas compared to inland areas that heat and cool more quickly.
Problem 2: Which pressure system usually brings rain and why?
Answer: Low pressure systems bring rain.
Why: In low pressure, air rises. As air rises, it cools. Cool air can't hold as much moisture, so water vapor condenses into clouds and precipitation forms.
Problem 3: Name two steps of the water cycle that are powered by the Sun's energy.
Answer:
  1. Evaporation: Sun heats water, turning liquid water into water vapor
  2. Transpiration: Sun causes plants to release water vapor

(The Sun also drives convection currents that move water vapor through the atmosphere)

Problem 4: Explain how the Sun, ocean, and atmosphere interact to create a hurricane.
Answer:
  1. Sun heats ocean water (‚â•26-27¬∞C)
  2. Warm ocean water evaporates ‚Üí moist air rises (hydrosphere ‚Üí atmosphere)
  3. As moist air rises and cools, water vapor condenses ‚Üí releases heat energy
  4. This heat powers the storm, causing more air to rise
  5. Earth's rotation organizes the system into a spinning storm
  6. The cycle continues as long as warm ocean water provides energy
Problem 5: Why are deserts often located under persistent high-pressure systems?
Answer: In high pressure systems, air sinks downward. As air sinks, it warms up and becomes drier (can hold more moisture). This prevents cloud formation and precipitation, creating dry desert conditions.

2. The Coriolis Effect:

Earth's rotation causes moving air and water to curve rather than travel in straight lines.

  • Northern Hemisphere: Wind curves to the RIGHT
  • Southern Hemisphere: Wind curves to the LEFT
  • This is why wind belts don't blow straight north-south
3. Jet Streams:

Fast-moving rivers of air high in the atmosphere (about 10 km up) that steer weather systems.

  • Flow from west to east in mid-latitudes
  • Separate cold polar air from warm tropical air
  • When jet stream dips south ‚Üí cold air invades
  • When jet stream moves north ‚Üí warm air moves in
  • Weather systems generally move with the jet stream
4. U.S. Weather Patterns:
  • The U.S. is located in the Westerlies wind belt (30-60¬∞N)
  • Weather systems generally move from west to east
  • The jet stream steers storms across the country
Remember: The U.S. is in the Westerlies, so weather moves WEST ‚Üí EAST. That's why meteorologists look at weather in California to predict weather in Texas!

  • When it moves north, warmer air moves in
  • Acts like a "highway" for weather systems

2. Ocean Currents:
  • Surface currents are driven by global winds
  • Currents transport heat around the planet
  • Example: Gulf Stream carries warm water from tropics to Europe, making Europe warmer than it would otherwise be
3. Hurricane Formation (Tropical Cyclones):

Hurricanes need specific conditions to form and strengthen:

  • Warm ocean water: At least 26-27¬∞C (about 80¬∞F) to provide energy
  • Moist air: Evaporation from warm ocean provides water vapor
  • Low wind shear: Winds at different altitudes must be similar so storm isn't torn apart
  • Coriolis effect: Earth's rotation organizes the storm into a spinning system
  • Distance from equator: At least 5¬∞ latitude (Coriolis effect too weak at equator)

4. Why Hurricanes Weaken:
  • Move over land: Lose warm ocean water energy source
  • Move over cold water: Not enough heat energy to sustain the storm
  • Friction with land: Terrain disrupts organized circulation
  • Dry air mixes in: Reduces moisture needed for condensation
  • Increased wind shear: Tears the storm apart
5. Hurricane Season:
  • Peak season: August - October (late summer/early fall)
  • Why? Ocean temperatures are warmest after summer heating
  • Wind shear is typically lower during this time

  • Trough: Lowest point of a wave
  • Wavelength (Œª): Distance from one crest to the next crest (or trough to trough)
  • Amplitude: Height from rest position to crest (or to trough)
  • Frequency (f): Number of waves that pass a point in one second (measured in Hertz, Hz)
